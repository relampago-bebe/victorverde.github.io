Mapping Populist Discourse Using Computational Methods

This project uses transformer-based word embeddings and semantic axis analysis to map the relational structure of political antagonism in large corpora of political speech. Rather than coding categories by hand, it leverages the geometry of language models to identify how political actors — elites, criminals, foreign powers, disloyal citizens — cluster together as "enemies of the people" through shared positions in semantic space.

The empirical case is Andrés Manuel López Obrador's presidency in Mexico, drawing on over 10 million words from his daily press conferences. The project contributes a computational hermeneutic approach that treats discourse not as a bag of words but as a structured field of relational contrasts — and shows that left-wing populist antagonism is a variable configuration of positions rather than a stable people-versus-elite divide.

Dual Citizenship in Ethnonational Populist Surges

This project examines what a second citizenship means — for the individuals who pursue it and for the nation-states that grant it. It asks how dual nationality functions not merely as a legal status but as a site where belonging, recognition, and political identity are negotiated under conditions of racialized insecurity and ethnonational populist pressure.

The empirical core is 52 in-depth interviews with second- and third-generation Mexican Americans who applied for Mexican citizenship between 2024 and 2025 — a surge coinciding with rising anti-immigrant politics in the United States and changes in Mexican nationality law. The interviews reveal a paradox of assimilation: individuals who have achieved conventional markers of incorporation into American society increasingly imagine mobility beyond it, not because assimilation failed, but because it instilled ideals of equality and recognition they now perceive as eroding.
